Everyone with a keyboard on Twitter nowadays is deliberating the last bulwark of human value in an AI world.
It's either taste or agency — the techarati can't quite decide which, but it's definitely one of them!

But are we (almost literally) missing the forest for the trees?
Individuals have traits, but we work in organizations.
And both agency and taste are worth approximately zero unless the organization has one thing — trust.

Imagine a hypothetical person, Agie, who is the highest agency person you've ever met, except they're 10x more "agentic" 🤮 than that.
They go into work and see that billing is a nightmare for sellers, so they go and rebuild it.
While they're building billing, they notice customer-facing usage data is slightly incorrect, so they submit a fix there.
While talking to the GTM team about their pain points, they realize pulling Intercom data into the CRM would save the GTM team, so they hook that up.

After a long day of fixing real, important problems for the business, Agie submits PRs for their work, logs off and takes a well-deserved hike up a mountain (Agie gonna Agie).
They wake up the next day and find, to their horror, their PR got absolutely wrecked in code review and their Slack is filled with messages from their manager chewing them out for not doing sprint work.

What happened here?
Agie exercised great judgement about what the business needed and applied a ton of agency to go and fix those problems — I don't see any problems there.
However, they made the mistake of applying agency in a low-trust organization where any kind of independent decision-making was second-guessed and their work was demeaned.
Agie had all the agency in the world, but the people around them didn't have enough trust to let them be effective.

You could imagine a similar situation with someone exercising good taste.
They apply a novel, yet tasteful, solution to a problem, but it gets watered down into something that is soulless because the organization didn't give it enough space to flourish.
I won't even paint more pixels on this since we've all seen it in our careers — good ol' death by committee.

So what is someone to do in a world where agency and taste are the two key human qualities left for professionals? (I have notes on this, but let's just take that statement at face value)
It means you absolutely must join a team that is high in trust and low in ego.
If you don't feel that you trust your coworkers, or your coworkers don't trust you, it's time to either get that fixed like yesterday or find a team where those things are true.

"Join a _new_ team to find trust? I don't trust Google Maps and you're telling me to switch to Apple Maps? I don't trust you on this," you might be saying.
And there's some truth to this.
Trust is built.
But there are at least two shortcuts:
  1. Join a team with people you've worked with previously that you trust. This is almost certainly the easiest way to operate in a high-trust environment.
  2. Join a team with a very high hiring bar where there is just implicit mutual trust.

It's a lot easier for the noisiest people on Twitter to lay the responsibility on individuals for AI-accelerated thrash since organizational dysfunction would imply their culpability as leaders.
But great leaders know that great teams are of course made up of great individuals, but those people must be able to trust each other to do the right thing.
